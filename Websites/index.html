What is a Commander?

In 1993, Andrew Garfield, under the company Wizards of the Coast, designed and published a tabletop game titled Magic: The Gathering. Often shortened simply to "Magic", the game steadily gained popularity and regularly received expansions and updates. The nature and popularity of the game allows for different playstyles and formats to form and florish. One format commonly played in Magic is known as Elder Dragon Highlander, shortened to EDH. EDH is a casual format that focuses on variance and interaction, with the staple of the format being the Commander card. The commander card is a legendary creature (Or vehicle, as of recent) the deckbuilder sets aside in the command zone. This card can be cast at any time the player can cast a creature with no need for the player to cross their fingers and hope they draw it. Due to this constant, there are additional rules to balance the decks that will presumably be built with the commander in mind. All legal decks have to follow these restrictions:

Color restriction

All cards in the deck must be within the color identity of the commander. If the chosen commander has red and blue color identities, then the deck can only have colorless, red, blue, and red blue spells. This limits the amount of staple cards and combination options available in the deck.

Number restriction

Excluding the commander card, the deck must have exactly 99 cards. This increases the unpredicability of the draw and introduces a tactic known as "deck thinning", where cards are placed in the deck simply to burn. The most common deck thinning card is called a "Fetch land", a land card that can be sacrificed to search for a land card.

Singleton restriciton

Unless a card states otherwise, commander decks can only have one instance of any card of the same name. This further reinforces the unpredictablity of a deck and encourages the use of lower power cards for redundancy. A deck that relies on a gimmick would, instead of putting several of the best gimmick support cards, play many cards that support the gimmick but aren't as "good" as the best cards.

I fell in love with the format because there are no decks that can win every single game they play. Even in the competetive scene, the meta is extremely varied and just three really good or really bad draws can drastically change a game. I discover a new way to win nearly every game and encounter new interactions often. I hope the commanders I list here inspire you to give the EDH format a shot, and remember: It's just cardboard, you don't need to buy a real Maha if you're just playing with your buddies, though I can't promise they won't get mad at you for playing Maha.

Admiral Brass, Unsinkable

I pray thee tell, WHAT is cooler than pirates? Having the color identity "Grixis", or red blue and black, Admiral Brass comes as the commander in a preconstructed deck released during the Lost Caverns of Ixilan set in 2023. This is why she was my first ever commander deck I owned. I had played a bit of another format before, but I was convinced to play other peoples' decks until I bought my own and boy was it worth it. Admiral Brass' whole schtick is ressurecting pirates. The first line of text on her card tells you to take the top four cards of your library and put them directly into the graveyard, otherwise known as "mill four." It seems counterintuitive at first, skipping the chance to draw into a card and throwing it away instead, but her second ability is the key. If she is on the battlefield, when you enter combat phase you are granted the ability to pluck a card with the creature type Pirate from the graveyard and slap it onto the battlefield with out paying it's mana cost. The card specifies the pirate gains haste, which, for brevity's sake, means unlike other cards it does not have to wait a turn to attack. Plus, it will have power and toughness 4/4 regardless of what the original card says. The only downside here is you can only do that once per pirate, dictated by the finality counter that Brass will place on your newly zombified pirate (ZOMBIE PIRATES!! SO COOL!!!!). If your zirate (zombie pirate, so cool) dies, it will not go the to graveyard but instead to the exile zone, where it essentially ceases to exist in the current game. The pirate cards that come with the deck are pretty dang good, and any pirate cards you could add are pretty cheap. The best part is, for a newcomer of the game, it has never been more okay to make mistakes. If you get a really cool pirate killed, everything is alright because it will come back even stroner next turn. It encourages you to learn risk assesment, when and what to attack with when combat comes, and just generally embodies the archetypes of each color in her color identity perfectly. Plus, pirates are all so fun. You take people's things, you make treasures, it's a very agressive deck in a non-agressive way. After a little but of editing on my end, this is one of my favorite decks to play when I don't want to make a plan. I always let the cards come to me, I figure it out on the fly, and it is extremely fun to play on your toes like that.

Rocco, Street Chef

Rocco, Street Chef utilizes a concept I breifly mentioned earlier that is usually a pain in the behind, the exile zone. With the color identity "Naya", or red white green, Rocco takes the top card of not only your library, but every other player's library as well. This card goes into exile, but instead of disappearing from the game entirely, it gives players a second chance to play this card before it disappears forever. This one turn limit on the exiled card encourages players to cast the card now rather than lose it, and this gives you two advantages. First, you get to see what your opponent is going to cast before they cast it, or even better see them not cast a card when it could have been really good to play later on. Second, if you look at Rocco's second ability he really likes when people cast the spells he pulled out. He celebrates the cast (or any cast from exile) by making food (which, among other niche things increases the player's life total) and increasing the power and toughness on a chosen creature. This deck is filled to the brim with shenanigans. I'm exiling as many cards as possible, and benifiting when I cast them through Rocco, or just enjoying the exile in the first place with cards like The War Doctor who is just happy to have a card exiled in the first place, he's not picky. I also love making food, which is has the type artifact token. These types bust the door wide open for various japes, as I can make them into mana, I can make them power, and other methods of horseplay. I play this deck a lot less agressively than I can. I see this deck as a way to make sure my friends are also playing cards since my exile is essentially a second draw per turn. So sometimes I fall victim to my own kind and generous and modest soul and I don't always win. When I do win though, I would like to reiterate the absolute tomfoolery I get into when I do.

Galadriel of Lothlórien

Oh my god is that the lady from Lord of The Rings? Anyways... Galadriel of Lothlórien was a gifted deck, and the person who gifted me the deck must think I'm awesome because this deck is really good. The card has a ringbearer ability that I'm sure is really cool if you build the deck around it, but I'm in the trenches of the second ability when I pilot this deck. Galadriel loves land cards, and she wants as many of them out NOW, not later. When you scry, an ability that allows you to look at the top card of your library and either keep it there or put it at the bottom of your library, Galadriel will go nuts if you scry a land card. She absolutley loses it and demands that you show everyone that you will get a land next draw. In fact, she will insist you go ahead and put that land onto the battlefield now. Being a "Simic" card, or a green blue card, she has a lot of land tricks up her magic sleeves. Seeing that lands produce mana, and cards need mana to be cast, getting more than one per turn is really really good. Whats really really even better is if those lands enter untapped, or in other words locked and loaded. Green has a lot of cards that like lands and blue has a lot of scrying, blue is THE wizard color. John Wizard is blue. Who else ponders their orb more than wizards? I'm rambling, but point stands. It is not hard to get either half of the engine RUNNING with this deck, and then you slam out a gaint creature (another green archetype) and win the game. If you don't scry a land card, you can always put the card at the bottom of your library and reveal the card anyway. If you reveal a land, you get it, if you don't, you are at a very slight disadvantage because now people know what you're going to draw. Gambling! I open magic packs, I love gambling!! My favorite thing about EDH, whether I win or not, is doing things. You almost never not do things in this deck. At least one thing per turn will likely happen. I've had decks where nothing happens for three or for turns a lot and it hurts so bad. This is a bandaid deck for those situations. Always fun, 10/10, thank you AJ for blessing me.
