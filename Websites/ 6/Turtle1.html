Intro

Having a pet turtle is a special thing. They’re not very difficult to take care of if bought full grown, having about as much intervention needs as a fish populated aquarium, but they’re more interactive and animated than fish. They’re quite hardy and easy to read, so they will offer you a good amount of grace while you learn your turtle’s needs. I got my turtle, a Stinkpot turtle I named Gaylord, as a rescue. He was full grown in a 10 gallon tank with 6 inches of water and no heat or basking area, so his survival is a testament to their hardiness I mentioned earlier (that being said please don’t do that to your new turtle!!) Me and Gaylord have had a heck of a time settling him into a proper habitat, as he had to learn how to live the lavish life of a loved house pet (he couldn’t swim when I got him) and I had to learn what the heck you do with a turtle. I’m here to share the knowledge I learned through my research and Gaylord studying to hopefully make the process easier for you!

Minimum Habitat Size

It is recommended, regardless of turtle species, to have 10 gallons per inch on your turtle’s shell. Gaylord came to me with a shell 5 inches long, so I bought a tank that can hold at least 50 gallons of water! Depending on the species of your turtle, you may want to consider dimensions when buying your tank. Your turtle will want to swim, so you want to make sure they have plenty of room to do it. A wide, stout tank will be better than a tall, long tank, but anything in between will work as well. Of course, you’re not limited to glass and acrylic tanks, and as long as you are able to follow all recommendations I list, whatever will work. If you have a bathtub you have no particular use for, you could very well plug it and rig up a fantastic turtle enclosure.

Climate Control-Water

There are a couple considerations to take when setting up the climate inside of your enclosure. First and foremost, you’ll need to control water temperature. As cold-blooded animals, turtles rely on their environment to maintain body temperature so this part is essential for a healthy happy turtle. Most turtles will be happy swimming in water that is between 75-85 degrees Fahrenheit, though you may want to double check your specific species needs. You will need a submersible water heater for this and you will need to make sure it is rated for your tank size. You'll need to treat the water you put into the tank with water conditioner if it's tap water, and regardless you should probably put an instant cylcer in if your first setting up your tank. If not, the bacteria and algae in the tank will run wild for the first week or two of the tank's life and it will be a nasty pain. You will next need a very good filter, as turtles are nasty. I’ve heard you should get a filter rated for a tank twice the tank’s size, but I’ve been doing well with ZooMed’s 50 gallon TurtleClean filter. Once again, I can confidently recommend it as the initial purchase isn’t too painful on the wallet and the replacements are easy to change, which you won’t need to do often if cleaned often. If you have the money, I can only assume your tank will be kept cleaner if you follow the tank size rule but I will never know because what I have works and I’m cheap.

Climate Control-Air

For the basking area, the hottest spot should register at 90 degrees Fahrenheit, though once again you may need to check your turtle’s specific needs. For this you will need a heat lamp, and the heat lamp should come with a guide as to the distance needed to warm the area to what degree. Speaking of basking areas, your turtle will also need some way to get Vitamin D–a mineral they get from the sun in the wild. We can’t exactly harness the sun in most enclosure situations, so a UVB light is necessary for their survival. I cannot stress this part enough, do NOT trust generic Amazon brands telling you the light has UVB. It does not. They lie for fun. The brand I trust is ZooMed’s ReptiSun, but you can also buy from Arcadia (I have heard very good about them) and Zilla. I believe Thrive is also a reputable brand, but I frankly haven’t heard enough to endorse them with my full chest, so do at your own discretion, I’m not sponsored here so I don’t care. I cannot stress how important finding a UVB lamp is as well as insuring calcium intake. These two things were neglected at the beginning of my journey with Gaylord and he very nearly died under my care.

Enrichment

Turtles are little bastards. To varying degrees of bastardness, your turtle will likely be causing problems wherever they can. They will bite, climb, push, and jump off of anything you put in that tank. That being said, it is very fun for them. I'm not entirely well versed on other turtles, but stinkpots are almost entirely aquatic, some never getting out to bask at all (you should always give them the option). To make your turtle happy, they should have little obstacles to climb on and jump off of. It's extremely cute watching them climb on something just to jump off after a minute! Besides climbing, they like to dabble in interior design and will move anything moveable. I cannot tell you how many times I've laid the tank out all nice just for Gaylord to decide the fake plant I put would go better on the other side of the tank. Speaking of plants, don't try real ones. It's not worth picking plant particles out.

Maintanence

Lovingly, I've learned from Gaylord that I will never get a water-borne pet again. It's not exactly a difficult process, keeping the tank clean, but it is messy. The bare minimum attention an aquarium should get is weekly water cycling, substrate cleaning, and biweekly filter cleanings. For water cycling, all you need to do is take out about a third of the water in the tank and replace it. You'll want to use water conditoner on the water you replace, a few drops per replaced gallon is all you need. It's likely some water evaporated during the week, so you'll want to account for that during the process. Substrate cleaning is done easily with a siphon vaccum, and is done easiest with an electric substrate vaccum. Turtle gunk needs to be removed from the substrate or it will seriously gunk up your tank, it's what makes turtles the most stinky to keep. Lastly, your filter will need to be cleaned and replaced pretty regularly. I cannot stress this enough, turtles are disgusting. They are evil on filters. At minimum, you'll need to clean the filter twice a month. I've made it a habit to rinse my filter with every water change since I need to shut it off anyways. You'll want to clean your filter with water from the tank because you actually want the bacteria that lives there to continue living there. Otherwise you'll experience algae blooms every single time you clean your filter which will actually make everything harder all the time!
